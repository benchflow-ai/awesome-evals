# Notes (workflow) — Benchmark ground-truth errors: (1) FutureHouse/Andrew White on Humanity's Last Exam (HLE) chemistry/biology; (2) Epoch AI FrontierMath Tiers 1-4 AI-assisted review and v2 corrections; (3) OpenAI retiring SWE-bench Verified.

**Author:** FutureHouse (Andrew D. White, co-founder & head of science) for the HLE piece; Epoch AI for FrontierMath; OpenAI for SWE-bench Verified
**URL:** https://www.futurehouse.org/research-announcements/hle-exam
**Found:** true

## Summary
Three independent 2025-2026 episodes converge on the same uncomfortable finding: expert-authored "frontier" benchmarks ship with ground-truth labels that are frequently wrong, and the errors are systematic rather than random noise. (1) HLE: FutureHouse audited the 321 text-only chemistry and biology questions of Humanity's Last Exam using their PaperQA2 literature agent (Crow), then had independent human chem/bio experts adjudicate a 150-question subset. They estimate 29 +/- 3.7% (95% CI) of these answers directly conflict with peer-reviewed literature, i.e. are likely wrong. They attribute the cause to the benchmark's construction incentive: questions had to be ones frontier models get wrong, which selected for adversarial 'gotcha' items and discouraged reviewers from verifying answers (reviewers were not required to verify if it took over five minutes). Scale AI (HLE's maker) responded by revising its preprint and reported an 18% error rate from its own check. (2) FrontierMath: Epoch AI ran an AI-assisted review of FrontierMath Tiers 1-4 that flagged 'fatal errors in about a third of problems,' most flags judged valid; they then shipped a corrected v2 on 2026-06-12 that addressed errors in 42% of problems (corrected 123 problems in Tiers 1-3 and 12 in Tier 4; removed 5 from Tiers 1-3 and 7 from Tier 4). Notably the original Tiers 1-4 'about' page had earlier claimed only ~1 in 20 (~5%) problems had errors 'comparable to other major ML benchmarks' — so the real rate was roughly 6-8x the self-reported estimate. (3) SWE-bench Verified: OpenAI stopped reporting on it after auditing 138 tasks that its model consistently failed across 64 independent runs and finding 59.4% were broken — 35.5% required a specific function name never mentioned in the problem, 18.8% checked for features not in the original problem — meaning the harness graded correct solutions as failures. OpenAI also found GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.5, and Gemini 3 Flash had all seen the benchmark's solutions in training (contamination), and pointed to SWE-bench Pro (models at ~70-80% on Verified drop to ~23% on Pro's public split).

## Key points
- HLE: 29 +/- 3.7% (95% CI) of the 321 text-only chemistry/biology HLE answers directly conflict with peer-reviewed literature; PaperQA2/Crow flagged conflicts, then independent human experts adjudicated 150 of the 321 outputs.
- Root cause is the golden-set construction incentive, not random error: questions had to be ones frontier models fail, producing adversarial 'gotcha' items; reviewers were not required to verify an answer if verification would exceed five minutes.
- Scale AI (HLE author) revised its preprint in response and reported an 18% error rate from its own check — so even the benchmark owner concedes a roughly 1-in-5 label error rate.
- FrontierMath: Epoch AI's AI-assisted review flagged 'fatal errors in about a third of problems,' most flags believed valid; the corrected v2 (2026-06-12) addressed errors in 42% of problems.
- FrontierMath v2 mechanics: corrected 123 problems in Tiers 1-3 and 12 in Tier 4; removed 5 from Tiers 1-3 and 7 from Tier 4; full dataset ~338 problems after correction.
- FrontierMath self-estimate was badly miscalibrated: the original Tiers 1-4 page claimed ~1-in-20 (~5%) error 'comparable to other ML benchmarks' — the AI-assisted review found ~6-8x more.
- SWE-bench Verified: OpenAI audited 138 tasks its model failed across 64 runs and found 59.4% broken — the benchmark was grading correct answers as wrong, not just being hard.
- Two distinct SWE-bench failure modes quantified: 35.5% of broken tasks demanded a specific function name never stated in the problem; 18.8% tested features absent from the original problem.
- SWE-bench Verified was also contaminated: GPT-5.2, Claude Opus 4.5, and Gemini 3 Flash had all seen its solutions in training; OpenAI now points to SWE-bench Pro, where Verified-high models fall to ~23%.
- Cross-cutting pattern: the harder and more 'expert'/'frontier' the benchmark, the worse the ground truth, because adversarial selection and review fatigue erode label quality exactly where verification is most expensive.

## What it adds
The book's P6 argues you need expert agreement before holding out a golden set, and P16 argues evals rot. This source supplies the hard, quantified evidence that the golden set itself is wrong on arrival — not just stale — even when authored by domain experts under marquee brands (Scale AI/HLE, Epoch, the SWE-bench team). It adds three concrete error rates from independent 2025-2026 audits (HLE ~29% / Scale's own 18%; FrontierMath ~33% flagged, 42% corrected; SWE-bench Verified 59.4% of failed tasks broken) and, crucially, a causal mechanism the book doesn't yet name: adversarial construction incentives ('must stump the model') plus review fatigue ('don't verify if it takes >5 min') actively manufacture bad labels. It also gives a self-calibration cautionary tale — Epoch's own pre-review estimate (~5%, 'comparable to other benchmarks') was 6-8x too low — and a worked example of label error masquerading as model failure (SWE-bench grading correct fixes as wrong), which is the precise nightmare P11/P3 warn about when a 'verifiable' reward is actually a broken verifier.

## Integration proposal
Use these as the anchor case studies in P6 and P16. In P6 ('Agree Before You Hold Out'), add a sidebar 'When the experts disagree with reality, not just each other' built on HLE: present the 29% (FutureHouse) vs 18% (Scale's own) numbers to show that even after expert authoring, a fifth to a third of labels are wrong, and name the failure mechanism — selecting items that stump models inverts the incentive away from correctness, and time-boxed review ('don't verify if >5 min') guarantees unchecked answers. State the rule: a golden set is not earned by expert authorship; it is earned by independent re-adjudication of the labels themselves, and you should budget an audit of items the best model fails before trusting any low score. In P16 ('Evals Rot'), add FrontierMath and SWE-bench Verified as the two flagship rot/error examples: FrontierMath shows self-reported error rates are untrustworthy (5% claimed vs ~33-42% found) and that AI-assisted re-review is a viable rot-detection tool; SWE-bench Verified shows the terminal stage — contamination plus broken graders — and the industry response (retire it, move to a harder held-out split where scores collapse from ~70-80% to ~23%). Tie SWE-bench's 'grading correct fixes as wrong' back to P11/P12: a verifier you haven't audited against known-correct solutions is itself an unmeasured judge. Concrete actionable takeaway box: (a) audit the labels on every item your strongest model fails; (b) re-derive your benchmark's error rate independently rather than trusting the authors'; (c) treat a sudden score collapse on a contamination-resistant variant as the signal that your headline benchmark has rotted.
